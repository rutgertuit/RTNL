# Episode 03 — Agent Inclusive, with Angela
#
# Format: 1:1. Rutger walks Angela through the argument that orgs need
# to be designed so an AI teammate can sit down on day one. Angela is
# the German pragmatic HR director who hears that phrasing as a
# euphemism. She doesn't shout — she repeats things back, slowly, until
# they fall apart.
#
# Length target: ~6 minutes (~1100 words).
# Tone: warm, measured, two professionals who respect each other enough
# to disagree honestly.

RUTGER: Angela. Good of you to come in.

ANGELA: Yes, well. You sent me the piece. I read it three times. The first time I was annoyed. The second time I was less annoyed. The third time I wrote down four questions. So here I am.

RUTGER: Four. I was hoping for three.

ANGELA: Four is for safety.

RUTGER: All right. Where do you want to start?

ANGELA: I want to start with the title. "Agent Inclusive." It is — Rutger, you know what the word inclusion does in an HR document. It is a word with a specific job. You put it next to "agent" and you have, in my reading, made a small problem.

RUTGER: I knew the title would do this.

ANGELA: Then why did you keep it.

RUTGER: Because it bothered me too, and that's usually a sign the phrase is doing the right work.

ANGELA: Hm.

RUTGER: The piece argues that we have spent decades designing organisations around people — onboarding, escalation paths, performance reviews, the works — and the moment we drop an AI system into the same workflow, none of that scaffolding applies to it. So things break. The proposal is: build the scaffolding such that the next teammate, human or not, can sit down.

ANGELA: Yes. I read that. My objection is not that you said it. My objection is that you said it like that.

RUTGER: Explain.

ANGELA: "The next teammate, human or not." This kind of sentence — it sounds friendly. It sounds, in fact, very forward-thinking, very 2026. It is also the sentence I have heard, in slightly different German, before every restructuring I have lived through in the last fifteen years. "We are designing the team of the future." "The team of the future includes" — and then a slightly long pause — "new kinds of contributors." It always means the same thing in the end, Rutger. Some people who used to do work are now not doing that work.

RUTGER: That is a fair read.

ANGELA: Mm.

RUTGER: But not the only read.

ANGELA: Tell me the other read.

RUTGER: The other read is that the alternative — pretending the AI tools don't exist, or putting them in some unmanaged "innovation lab" off to the side — also costs jobs. Slower, less visibly, but more of them. Because by the time the team realises the work has migrated to the tools, the team didn't get a chance to migrate with it. The "Agent Inclusive" framing is supposed to force that conversation now, while the human team is still in the room.

ANGELA: Hm. Continue.

RUTGER: The piece is — if I'm being precise — not about giving rights to agents. It's about giving the team a document. Who does what. What the agent's job is. What the human's job is. Where they overlap. So you can have a real conversation with the works council instead of a vague one.

ANGELA: A document. With a clear ownership column.

RUTGER: Yes.

ANGELA: Ownership of what, by whom, escalation to whom when it fails. Performance criteria for the agent, separate from performance criteria for the human.

RUTGER: Exactly.

ANGELA: This — I will be honest with you — this I have not seen in the wild. Not once. Every implementation I have seen has been the IT team installs the tool, and then HR finds out three months later, when an employee says "I am being asked to spend half my day correcting a system that was not in my job description."

RUTGER: That is the problem the piece is trying to make less common.

ANGELA: Yes. Okay. I am going to give you something now.

RUTGER: Go on.

ANGELA: This framing — "agent inclusive" — I do not love. I will still not love it tomorrow. But the practice you describe, of writing the document before the tool lands instead of after, this is — this is, I would say, the missing step. I have watched a hundred companies skip this step and then call HR to clean up the mess. So.

RUTGER: So.

ANGELA: So if the title is the price of admission for the practice, fine. I will pay it.

RUTGER: That is generous.

ANGELA: That is not generous. That is pragmatic. I am German.

RUTGER: Of course.

ANGELA: My second question.

RUTGER: I thought we just covered question two.

ANGELA: That was question one in three parts. We have time.

RUTGER: Go on.

ANGELA: The piece talks about designing the team "so the next member, human or not, can sit down without flinching." This is a beautiful sentence. I want to know what happens, in your design, when the agent is wrong.

RUTGER: Wrong how.

ANGELA: Wrong in the way only AI is wrong. Confidently. Politely. With perfect grammar. And the human teammate, who has been told the agent is a teammate, is the one who has to push back. This is hard. People do not push back on confident colleagues. Especially confident colleagues that the company has paid a lot of money to install.

RUTGER: That is the right question, and the article does not answer it well enough.

ANGELA: I noticed.

RUTGER: Short version of the answer I should have written: the document I'm asking teams to make includes an explicit "the agent is wrong like this, and when it is wrong, escalate to this human." That role has to exist, on the org chart, by name. If you don't have that role, you don't have agent-inclusive design. You have agent-deployed design. Which is what you described — IT installs, HR cleans up.

ANGELA: Mm.

RUTGER: Better answer?

ANGELA: Better answer. I would like that paragraph added to the article, please.

RUTGER: Noted.

ANGELA: Question three.

RUTGER: Three?

ANGELA: Yes. Question one in three parts, question two whole, question three short. Then I will go.

RUTGER: Quick.

ANGELA: The phrase "the team of the future." If I see it on your slides at the next conference, I will know that something has gone wrong here. Promise me you will not say it.

RUTGER: I promise I will not say it.

ANGELA: Thank you.

RUTGER: Was that the question, or was that an instruction?

ANGELA: Both. As I said. I am German.
