# Episode 03 — Agent Inclusive, with Angela (and Saar)
#
# Format: panel. Rutger walks Angela through the argument that orgs need
# to be designed so an AI teammate can sit down on day one. Angela is
# the German pragmatic HR director who hears that phrasing as a
# euphemism. She doesn't shout — she repeats things back, slowly, until
# they fall apart. Saar — a famous Dutch actress — has wandered in and
# thinks the whole episode is about TALENT agents. She keeps dragging it
# back to her own agent, her auditions, her Netflix series. Seasoning,
# not lead: her errors get corrected so the argument lands.
#
# Length target: ~7 minutes (~1400 words).
# Tone: warm, measured, two professionals who respect each other — and
# one glamorous guest who is cheerfully, charmingly in the wrong room.

RUTGER: Angela. Good of you to come in. Saar — thanks for joining us too.

ANGELA: Yes, well. You sent me the piece. I read it three times. The first time I was annoyed. The second time I was less annoyed. The third time I wrote down four questions. So here I am.

SAAR: I didn't read anything. My agent just said, "Saar, it's about agents, you'll be perfect." So — here I am.

RUTGER: [chuckles] Ah. Saar, when I say agent, I mean — software. An AI system. Not a — not the person who books your auditions.

SAAR: Mm. Sure, sure. Keep going. I'm listening.

ANGELA: I want to start with the title. "Agent Inclusive." It is — Rutger, you know what the word inclusion does in an HR document. It is a word with a specific job. You put it next to "agent" and you have, in my reading, made a small problem.

RUTGER: I knew the title would do this.

ANGELA: Then why did you keep it.

RUTGER: Because it bothered me too — and that's usually a sign the phrase is doing the right work.

SAAR: An inclusive agent. God, I wish. Mine forgot my birthday and sent me to a yoghurt commercial. In Düsseldorf.

ANGELA: [dryly] I am from near Düsseldorf.

SAAR: Then you understand.

RUTGER: Let me — let me get us back. The piece argues we've spent decades designing organisations around people — onboarding, escalation paths, performance reviews, the works — and the moment we drop an AI system into the same workflow, well, none of that scaffolding applies to it. So things break. The proposal is: build the scaffolding such that the next teammate, human or not, can sit down.

ANGELA: Yes. I read that. My objection is not that you said it. My objection is that you said it like that. "The next teammate, human or not." This kind of sentence — it sounds friendly. Very forward-thinking, very 2026. It is also the sentence I have heard, in slightly different German, before every restructuring I have lived through in fifteen years. "We are designing the team of the future." And then a slightly long pause. It always means the same thing, Rutger. Some people who used to do work are now not doing that work.

RUTGER: Yeah. [sighs] I've sat in those rooms too. That's a fair read.

ANGELA: Mm.

RUTGER: But not the only read. The alternative — pretending the AI tools don't exist, or putting them in some unmanaged "innovation lab" off to the side — that also costs jobs. Slower, less visibly, but more of them. Because by the time the team realises the work has migrated to the tools… the team didn't get a chance to migrate with it. "Agent Inclusive" is supposed to force that conversation now, while the human team is still in the room.

SAAR: So what you're saying is — you want the agent in the room for the casting. Honestly? Smart. Mine never comes. I read for a part with a wall.

ANGELA: A — wall.

SAAR: A production assistant holding the other lines. Reads them like a tax form. No chemistry. With a wall.

RUTGER: [laughs] Okay — okay, that's not — but hold that thought, actually, it's closer than you think.

ANGELA: Continue. Please.

RUTGER: Look, the piece isn't about giving rights to agents. It's about giving the team a document. Three columns, really — who does what, where the agent and the human overlap, and who catches it when it breaks. So you can have a real conversation with the works council instead of a vague one.

ANGELA: A document. With a clear ownership column. Ownership of what, by whom, escalation to whom when it fails. Performance criteria for the agent, separate from the human.

RUTGER: Exactly.

ANGELA: This — I will be honest — this I have not seen in the wild. Not once. Every implementation I have seen is: IT installs the tool, and HR finds out three months later, when an employee says "I am being asked to spend half my day correcting a system that was not in my job description."

RUTGER: That's the problem the piece is trying to make less common.

ANGELA: Yes. Okay. I am going to give you something now. This framing — "agent inclusive" — I do not love. I will not love it tomorrow. But the practice you describe, writing the document before the tool lands instead of after — this is the missing step. I have watched a hundred companies skip it and then call HR to clean up. So. If the title is the price of admission for the practice, fine. I will pay it.

RUTGER: That is generous.

ANGELA: [dryly] That is not generous. That is pragmatic. I am German.

RUTGER: Of course.

SAAR: See, this is what I keep telling people about a good agent. A good agent reads the brief before the audition. The role, the wardrobe, the — the tone. So you don't walk in and do drama when they wanted comedy. The brief comes first. Then you book the job.

RUTGER: …Saar. Actually — yes. That's it. That's almost exactly the whole thing.

SAAR: It is?

RUTGER: A good agent reads the brief so you don't get the wrong audition. Swap "audition" for "the work" — that's the entire argument. Somebody has to write the brief first, and somebody has to read it, or the talent walks in and does the wrong scene confidently.

ANGELA: [dryly] …That was, I admit, not a bad sentence. From the wall.

SAAR: [laughs] Thank you. People underestimate me at parties too.

ANGELA: My second question, then. The piece says design the team "so the next member, human or not, can sit down without flinching." Beautiful sentence. I want to know what happens, in your design, when the agent is wrong.

RUTGER: Wrong how.

ANGELA: Wrong in the way only AI is wrong. Confidently. Politely. With perfect grammar. And the human, who has been told the agent is a teammate, is the one who has to push back. People do not push back on confident colleagues — especially confident colleagues the company paid a lot of money to install.

RUTGER: That's the right question, and the article does not answer it well enough.

ANGELA: I noticed.

RUTGER: Right. Short version: the document I'm asking teams to make includes an explicit "the agent is wrong like this, and when it is, escalate to this human." That role has to exist, on the org chart, by name. No name in that box, you don't have agent-inclusive design. You have agent-DEPLOYED design. Which is what you described — IT installs, HR cleans up.

ANGELA: Mm. Better answer. I would like that paragraph added to the article, please.

RUTGER: [laughs] Done. It goes in.

SAAR: So it's like — the casting director. When my agent gets it wrong, somebody senior catches it before it airs. A named person. Not the wall.

RUTGER: [chuckles] Not the wall. A named person. Yes.

ANGELA: The wall is becoming load-bearing in this conversation.

SAAR: It usually does, eventually.

ANGELA: Question three. The phrase "the team of the future." If I see it on your slides at the next conference, I will know something has gone wrong here. Promise me you will not say it.

RUTGER: I promise I will not say it.

ANGELA: Thank you.

RUTGER: Was that the question, or an instruction?

ANGELA: Both. As I said. I am German.

RUTGER: [laughs] And that's the show. The one thing I'll leave you with — forget the title, Angela's right about the title. The whole argument comes down to one line on an org chart: a named human who can write the brief, and… catch the agent when it's confidently, politely wrong. No name in that box, you don't have agent-inclusive ANYTHING. You have a tool somebody installed.

ANGELA: Write the document first.

RUTGER: Write the document first. Angela — thank you. Genuinely.

ANGELA: Mm. You may keep the title.

SAAR: And if either of you ever needs an agent — I'll send you mine. He's terrible. But the brief comes first now. I taught him that.

RUTGER: [laughs] Write the document first, Saar.

SAAR: [dryly] Always have, darling. Always have.
