# Multiplier Myth — the conversation (rebuilt)
#
# Cast: RUTGER (host/lead — carries the argument from the article) +
# ORACLE (strategy / measurement character — defends the efficiency,
# headcount-math view, then gets reframed) + SAAR (comic foil — famous
# Dutch actress, raspy/seductive, clueless; filters everything through
# showbiz: "multiplier" / "the work that compounds" → a franchise, sequels,
# residuals). Companion to the article at /business/multiplier-myth.
#
# The article's spine: AI is a multiplier only if you point it at the work
# that compounds, not as a way to do the same job with fewer people. The
# fresh-beyond-the-article angle here is the SECOND-ORDER cost — the part
# you save by cutting heads is the part that was teaching the next idea.
#
# Saar's hook: she hears "the work that compounds" as the SEQUEL — shoot once,
# pays forever — and misquotes the cost case as "fire everyone and buy a
# microwave." Oracle is exasperated; Rutger redirects. One beat: her
# residuals misread accidentally nails it — the compounding work is the
# asset that keeps paying, not the disposable one-off.
#
# Voice: Rutger per docs/podcasts/rutger-voice.md. Oracle precise, pompous,
# builds decks. Saar = seasoning, errors corrected so the point lands.
# Personal views; Rutger never speaks for Google. No retired terms.
#
# Length target: ~1300-1500 words.

RUTGER: Oracle. So — you read the piece. You hated it.

ORACLE: I did not hate it. I disagreed with it, which is a more expensive service. I have brought a deck.

RUTGER: [chuckles] We don't share screens on audio.

ORACLE: I am aware. I have brought it in spirit. Slide one is a single number. The number is the cost of one full-time employee, fully loaded, in this market. Slide two is the same number, multiplied by the headcount a competent agent can now cover.

SAAR: Multiplied. Mm. I love a number that multiplies. That's a franchise, darling. You shoot the one film, and then it just — pays, and pays, and pays. [chuckles]

ORACLE: …That is not what the slide says.

RUTGER: [laughs] Saar, hang on — let me get you in properly. Everyone, Saar's sitting in today. She, uh — she has opinions.

SAAR: I have residuals.

RUTGER: She has residuals. Okay. Oracle, finish the thought — your read's the obvious one. AI arrives, you do the same work with fewer people, you bank the difference.

ORACLE: My read is the one a board will actually approve. It is legible. It is dated. It survives an audit. "We reduced cost of delivery by eleven percent" is a sentence a CFO can sign. "We expanded our cognitive bandwidth" is a sentence that gets you walked out of the room.

SAAR: So what you're saying is — you want to fire everyone and buy a microwave.

ORACLE: I beg your —

RUTGER: [laughs] Okay, that's — that's almost the analogy, weirdly. Hold that, Saar, you're early but you're not wrong. Oracle, the kitchen. Have you ever watched a real kitchen during service? A proper brigade.

ORACLE: I have watched the bill.

RUTGER: Then picture the line. You've got commis chefs doing prep, a chef de partie running each station, a sous-chef holding the whole pass together. The expensive question is not "how few people can plate a dish." You can plate a dish with two people and a microwave.

SAAR: There it is. The microwave. I said the microwave.

ORACLE: [dryly] You did. The room is grateful.

RUTGER: The expensive question is how many covers you can turn at quality, on a Friday, when the room is full and the orders are screaming. That's the number that compounds. More covers, better word of mouth, a fuller room next Friday.

ORACLE: And AI is the microwave.

RUTGER: No — no, that's the whole point. AI is not the microwave. The cost-cutter thinks it's a microwave: same dish, fewer hands. Used properly it's a second pass, a third station, a prep team that never sleeps. It doesn't let you serve the same forty covers with two chefs. It lets the same brigade turn a hundred and forty without the food falling apart.

ORACLE: That is a lovely image. It is also — a story a vendor tells you right before the invoice.

RUTGER: [chuckles] Go on. Push.

ORACLE: Here is the real case, and I want you to take it seriously, because it is not a strawman. A board does not get to spend imaginary covers. It spends real euros, this quarter. The "more markets, more covers" upside is a forecast. The headcount saving is a fact. When you ask a finance committee to choose between a fact and a forecast, the fact wins, and it should. That is not a failure of imagination. That is fiduciary duty.

SAAR: So what you're saying is the sequel hasn't been greenlit yet, but the layoffs have. [chuckles]

ORACLE: [sighs] …I would not have phrased it as a sequel.

RUTGER: No, but — Saar, that's actually clean. That's the tension exactly. The cut is signed; the upside is still in development.

ORACLE: It gets stronger. Your own article admits the savings story is real for the clerical layer. Spreadsheets did remove bookkeepers. ATMs did lower tellers per branch. So when I cut, I am not hallucinating. I am reading the same history you are.

RUTGER: You are. And — here's where I think you and I actually agree more than it looks. The cut is real. What I'd argue is the cut is not the strategy — it's the table stakes. Everybody gets the eleven percent. Your competitor with the same tool gets the same eleven percent. So you've spent your one big move on a thing the whole market also did. You're back to even, just smaller.

ORACLE: Smaller and solvent.

RUTGER: Smaller and identical. Which in a fast market is its own risk. But — let me give you the part the article doesn't say. The part I, I keep coming back to lately.

SAAR: Is this the bit where I cry. I do a wonderful single tear.

RUTGER: [laughs] Save the tear. When you cut the junior layer to bank the saving, you're not just removing cost. You're removing the place the next idea was going to come from. The commis chef who's annoying and slow this year is the chef de partie who reinvents the menu in three years. The cost you cut is also the apprenticeship. AI is brilliant at the task. It is TERRIBLE at growing the person who has the taste.

SAAR: Oh, but that's the residual. That's the whole point of a residual, darling — you keep the thing that keeps paying you, and you let go of the thing you only shoot once. The franchise pays forever. The microwave doesn't pay anyone.

RUTGER: …Saar. That's actually it.

ORACLE: [dryly] I find that deeply irritating.

RUTGER: The compounding work — the thing that keeps paying — that's the asset you protect. The one-off task is the part you'd automate. You don't sell off the franchise to save on the catering. She just said the whole article in showbiz.

SAAR: I always say the article. Nobody ever tells me which one.

ORACLE: …That is a second-order effect. Boards do not price second-order effects.

RUTGER: No, they don't. That's exactly why it's the expensive mistake. It doesn't show up on this quarter's slide. It shows up four years later as "why don't we have anyone who can run this," and by then the saving is long spent and nobody connects the two.

ORACLE: You are describing a bill that arrives after the person who saved the money has been — promoted.

RUTGER: [laughs] That is the most honest sentence anyone's said about transformation all year.

ORACLE: I bill extra for honesty. So fine. Suppose I grant you the apprenticeship point. What do I actually do differently on Monday. Decks need an action slide.

RUTGER: Three things. And, well, they're cheap — which you'll like.

ORACLE: I do like cheap.

RUTGER: One — before you point AI at any job, ask whether that job compounds. Does doing more of it make the next thing easier? Sales pipeline compounds. Filing expense reports… does not. Point the multiplier at the compounding work first.

ORACLE: One. Good. Two.

RUTGER: Two — protect the apprenticeship on purpose. If the agent now does what your juniors used to learn on, give the juniors the harder thing the agent can't do yet. Don't let the ladder lose its bottom rung by accident.

ORACLE: Two. Reluctantly noted. Three.

RUTGER: Three — measure the surface area, not just the cost line. Not "how many hours did we save," but "what did the team go after this quarter that they couldn't reach last quarter." If the answer is nothing… you bought a microwave.

SAAR: The microwave again. It's a motif now. I respect a motif.

ORACLE: Compounding work, the apprenticeship, the surface area. Three. I can build a deck on three. I cannot build a deck on a kitchen metaphor.

RUTGER: You can. You just won't put the kitchen in the deck.

ORACLE: I will put the kitchen in the deck and attribute it to myself.

RUTGER: [laughs] Obviously. Give me your honest landing, though. Where do you actually still disagree.

ORACLE: I still think you underweight the quarter. The forecast is real, the apprenticeship is real — but rent is also real, and some businesses do not get four years. For those, the cut is not a mistake. It is survival.

RUTGER: That's right. And, yeah — I'd take that. The myth isn't "never cut." The myth is "the cut is the WIN." For a healthy business sitting on a multiplier and choosing to spend it shrinking — that's the expensive one.

ORACLE: Then we have a smaller disagreement than your article implies. How disappointing for both of us.

SAAR: So nobody's getting fired? Or everybody is, but tastefully?

ORACLE: [sighs] Nobody is getting fired, Saar. We are agreeing.

SAAR: God, how dull. No third act at all.

RUTGER: It's the best outcome two people can hope for. We started further apart and we're leaving slightly closer, which is the whole reason to talk instead of post.

ORACLE: A sentiment I will be billing you for.

RUTGER: [chuckles] Of course you will. Last thing, for the record — none of this is a Google position. It's my read, on my site, with synthetic voices. Oracle here is a character and an invoice. Saar is — Saar.

SAAR: Saar is available for the right project.

RUTGER: That's our button. Go point the multiplier at the work that compounds — keep the residual, lose the microwave — and keep the commis chef. They're the next menu. Goodnight.
