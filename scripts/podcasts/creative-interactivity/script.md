# Episode 04 — Interactivity Is The New Explanation, panel
#
# Format: 2-guest panel. Rutger + Oracle (strategy consultant, defends
# slides) + Marie (research scientist, lands short precision
# interjections). Oracle dominates the time; Marie keeps everyone honest
# with one-line definitional pushes.
#
# Length target: ~6-7 minutes (~1200 words).
# Tone: light, sparring, the kind of dinner-party argument three people
# enjoy having even though no one is going to fully convince the others.
#
# IMPORTANT for the renderer: Marie's lines are deliberately short to
# keep her voice easy to follow. Do not lengthen them in production.

RUTGER: Welcome both. Oracle, you have a deck. Marie, you have, what, two questions?

MARIE: One. With a follow-up.

ORACLE: I have a deck. I always have a deck. I have brought it in spirit only — Rutger, I respect your no-screens policy on the podcast.

RUTGER: We have screens. We just don't share them on audio.

ORACLE: A small mercy.

RUTGER: Let me set the stage. I argued in a piece that interactive artefacts — a live tool, a small playable thing, a hands-on demo — beat slides for explaining anything new. The article had two mini-games on it as exhibits. Oracle, you read it. Reaction.

ORACLE: My reaction is that you are writing for a very specific audience. An audience that does not run a publicly listed company.

RUTGER: Go on.

ORACLE: A slide is, before anything else, a unit of accountability. It is signed off, dated, distributed, archived, and — when it is eventually wrong — the meeting where it was approved can be reconstructed. You cannot do that with a live demo. A live demo is a *performance*. Performances are wonderful. They are not minutes.

RUTGER: That's a fair distinction.

MARIE: Define accountability.

ORACLE: I beg your pardon?

MARIE: You said the slide is the unit of accountability. Accountability for what.

ORACLE: For the decision the deck recommended.

MARIE: Hmm. Okay.

ORACLE: You disagree.

MARIE: I disagree that the slide is accountable. The slide is a record of a recommendation. The decision is somewhere else. Usually in an email afterwards.

ORACLE: That is a very European distinction.

MARIE: I am very European.

RUTGER: I want to defend Oracle for a moment. There is something he is right about. A slide is portable. You can email a slide. You can put a slide in a board pack. You cannot email a hover state.

ORACLE: Thank you, Rutger. I was beginning to feel ambushed.

MARIE: You were not ambushed. You were questioned.

ORACLE: In my experience the second is a subset of the first.

RUTGER: But — here is where I push back. The piece is not arguing that slides should go away. It is arguing that for *new concepts*, especially AI workflows, the deck loses the audience faster than the live tool would. The audience needs to *touch the idea*. Then we can write the slide together afterwards.

ORACLE: Yes. This part of the piece I do not actually disagree with. I just think it is fragile in the wild.

RUTGER: Fragile how.

ORACLE: A live demo only works if it works. If the wifi is shaky, if the API rate-limits you at minute three, if you forget the prompt you rehearsed — you have nothing. A slide does not have rate limits. A slide opens.

RUTGER: That is a real cost.

MARIE: One question.

RUTGER: Marie?

MARIE: When the demo fails — what does the audience actually take away? In the failure case.

ORACLE: They take away that the technology is not ready.

MARIE: Even when the failure is the wifi.

ORACLE: Especially when the failure is the wifi.

RUTGER: That is bleak.

MARIE: It is also true.

ORACLE: This is why I keep my decks.

RUTGER: Okay, fair. But — and this is the thing I want to land — the asymmetry has shifted. Five years ago a live AI demo had a thirty percent chance of working in a boardroom. Today it has ninety. The wifi is the only weak link, and Eduroam is improving.

ORACLE: A bold claim about Eduroam.

RUTGER: I stand by it.

MARIE: I have my follow-up.

RUTGER: Go.

MARIE: The piece says interactivity is the new explanation. I want to know — what does interactivity stop explaining? Because every new format gives up something. The deck gave up motion. The video gave up density. What does the live demo give up.

RUTGER: That is the right question. It is the one I kept circling and never quite pinned in the piece.

ORACLE: I will give Rutger a moment to enjoy being caught out.

RUTGER: [laughs] I will take the moment. Here is the honest answer. The live demo gives up the *frozen argument*. A deck has one real advantage — you cannot edit it once it is sent. The recipient reads the same nine words on slide seven that you wrote, in the order you wrote them. A live demo is rewritten every time you give it. Sometimes better, often worse, but it is never the same artefact twice.

MARIE: Hmm. Yes. So the demo gains involvement, it loses fidelity of message.

RUTGER: Exactly. That is the sentence the piece was missing.

ORACLE: Marie, would you like a job at my firm.

MARIE: I have one already.

ORACLE: I am offering you a better one.

MARIE: You do not know that. You have not seen my current job.

RUTGER: Let me close. Oracle, give me the bumper sticker. When is a slide the right answer.

ORACLE: When the decision needs to outlive the meeting. When the audience cannot interact in the moment. When the artefact will be forwarded twice. Three rules. I do not give them away cheaply.

RUTGER: And Marie.

MARIE: A demo when the audience needs to feel it. A slide when they need to remember it. The article should add this sentence.

RUTGER: Noted.

ORACLE: I will be billing you for these insights, Rutger.

RUTGER: I assumed. And I will pay it, because here is what we actually landed. The slide and the demo are not rivals — they are the trade-off. The demo lets the room *touch* the idea and walk out involved. The slide freezes the argument so it survives the walk to the next meeting. You give one up to get the other. That is the whole piece, and I needed both of you to say it out loud.

MARIE: A demo to convince the room. A slide to convince the room they were not there.

ORACLE: …I am going to use that. On a slide.

RUTGER: [laughs] Then it is yours. Send me the invoice — and the deck.
