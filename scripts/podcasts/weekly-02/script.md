RUTGER: Week two. Dino and Angela this time. The topic is Brandcast, which is what YouTube calls its upfront. For anyone outside the US media industry — an upfront is the thing television networks do every May where they stand on a stage and try to convince advertisers to commit their annual budgets before the season starts. The pitch is: look how many people watch us, look how good the content is, buy the year. YouTube did this at Lincoln Center on the thirteenth. Trevor Noah hosted. Chappell Roan performed. And the announcement I want to talk about was not the headliner on stage. It was in the press release. Maya.

DINO: I knew this was going to be about a press release.

RUTGER: It is always about the press release.

DINO: Fine. What did the press release say.

RUTGER: It said that YouTube is adding a checkout button to the television. The product is called Buy with Google Pay. If you are watching a creator video on your connected television, and the creator has tagged a product, you can now buy it in two clicks without leaving the video. The transaction completes on your TV. Google Pay handles it. The merchant stays merchant of record.

ANGELA: That is the Universal Cart from the ad stack, but on the content side.

RUTGER: That is exactly what it is. And that is the sentence I want every listener to hold. In the episode before this one, we talked about how the agentic ad stack closes the loop on the brand side — Conversational Discovery Ads, Universal Cart, Agent Payments Protocol. The platform becomes an agent that talks to the shopper and then transacts. What Brandcast announced is the same loop, but from the creator's side. You are watching a person you trust talk about a product. The screen notices. You tap twice. Done.

DINO: You are describing a shopping channel.

RUTGER: I am describing a shopping channel where you chose to watch the person on it.

DINO: That is a meaningful distinction.

RUTGER: It is the only distinction. And it is what the four-surface doctrine has been saying since I started writing on this site. YouTube is television and social and search and shopping — not four separate strategies, one surface behaving differently depending on what the viewer needs in that moment. The checkout button is not an add-on. It is the last surface arriving in the room where the other three were already living.

DINO: I want to push on the trust part. The Brandcast pitch was built around creator shows — Alex Cooper, Trevor Noah, Kareem Rahma with Keep the Meter Running. Premium content, premium stage, Lincoln Center. That framing is designed to make the shopping feel native. But the shopping has been there for a while in a more awkward form. The trust is not new. The infrastructure is new.

RUTGER: Yes. And that is an honest distinction. The creator-audience trust dynamic is not a 2026 invention. What is new is that the infrastructure has finally caught up to what the trust makes possible. The number YouTube cited in the public materials: when creators talk about products on YouTube, viewers are thirteen times more likely to search for the brand and five times more likely to buy. That number has been building for years. What changed is that the path from "I trust this person" to "I own this thing" is now two clicks on a television. The friction went to near zero.

ANGELA: What does that mean for how a brand should think about the upfront now.

RUTGER: That is the question.

ANGELA: It means the creator brief is not a brand safety form anymore. If a creator's recommendation is thirteen times more likely to drive a search, and the checkout converts that on the spot, the brief you give the creator is effectively a product pitch. Every word the creator uses — the specific comparison they make, the use case they describe, the objection they wave off — now lands in a context where the viewer can act immediately. There is no longer a gap between awareness and consideration. You have to write for the moment of purchase, not the moment of attention.

RUTGER: Which maps back to the Jazz Swing. The AI — the product tagging, the checkout integration, the recommendation layer — handles the rigid infrastructure. The swing is the creator's judgment. What they choose to say and how they say it. And now, behind that swing, the whole transaction infrastructure is loaded and ready.

DINO: That is a flattering way to describe a shopping channel.

RUTGER: I am not pretending it is not a shopping channel. I am saying that the best shopping channels have always worked because a person you trusted was doing the talking. QVC built a whole economy on that. YouTube is the same mechanic, at a scale QVC could not have imagined, with a trust dynamic the creator and the audience built together before the advertiser arrived.

ANGELA: The operational note I would add: Custom Sponsorships — also announced at Brandcast, it is in the Google blog post — uses AI to surface videos that match a brand's desired contextual moment. A brand can now find the creator who is already talking about the problem their product solves, and surface a sponsorship. They are not buying reach anymore. They are buying context, from a creator who already established the trust.

RUTGER: And the machine is doing the matching. The human still has to decide what the right context is, which creators reflect the brand honestly, which sponsorship terms are defensible. The brief again. Always the brief.

DINO: If one more person says "brief" I will write a brief about it.

RUTGER: Please do. Document it. Send it to Angela.

ANGELA: I will turn it into a framework with numbered bullet points.

RUTGER: Three things a brand should do now. Angela.

ANGELA: One: review your creator brief as a purchase brief, not an awareness brief, because the checkout is live on television. Two: audit which creator conversations already mention your product category, because the Custom Sponsorships AI will surface those organically and you want to arrive with a real position, not a last-minute slot-buy. Three: connect your product feed to the creator commerce layer before the budget cycle closes — the infrastructure exists, the friction is low, and the brands who wait for a proof case will be watching a competitor's two-click-on-a-television story at next year's Brandcast.

RUTGER: That last one is the Multiplier Myth in a single sentence. The leverage is in doing it while the infrastructure is new and the cost is still low. Not in waiting for someone else to prove it works.

DINO: It only works if the creator actually believes in the product.

RUTGER: That is the non-negotiable. The trust is real. You cannot manufacture it by buying the slot. You have to be the product a creator would honestly recommend.

DINO: Which means the product also has to be worth recommending.

RUTGER: Now we are in a different podcast.

DINO: Maybe next week.
