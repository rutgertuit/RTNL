# Episode 06 — How To Build A Character Sheet, panel
#
# Format: 2-guest panel. Rutger + Dino (creative dinosaur, defends the
# "soul" of a character — longer, irritable, blunt) + Marie (research
# scientist, defines consistency with precision — short bursts).
#
# The article says: a consistent AI character library is the highest-
# leverage move in any AI image workflow that uses a recurring person.
# Dino's pushback: a consistent face is not a character. Marie's
# pushback: define consistency, mathematically. They agree at the end
# in ways neither expected.
#
# Length target: ~6-7 minutes (~1200 words).
# Tone: dinner-party argument. Marie's lines stay short.

RUTGER: Welcome both. Dino, Marie. Have you two met.

DINO: Briefly. At a thing.

MARIE: At Eindhoven. The thing where you spilled wine on the curator.

DINO: That was the curator's fault.

MARIE: The curator was three metres away.

DINO: Three metres in art-school metres is one metre in real metres.

RUTGER: Let me start before we lose the audience. I wrote a piece called How To Build A Character Sheet. About making a consistent AI character for image work. The argument is that the *single highest-leverage piece of prep* in any AI image pipeline that involves a recurring person is having a small set of reference shots of that character. Same person, six angles. From that, everything else gets cheaper.

DINO: The argument is wrong.

MARIE: The argument is incomplete.

RUTGER: That is two different kinds of disagreement and I want both. Dino first.

DINO: A character is not a face. A character is a way of holding the hands. A character is the small thing the eyes do when something embarrassing happens. A character is whether they sit down too close to the door at a restaurant. A face is, at best, ten percent of the information. You build a character sheet of six faces and you have built a *catalogue*. You have not built a person.

RUTGER: That is a real distinction.

DINO: That is the only distinction. Everything else is hardware.

MARIE: Define consistent.

RUTGER: Marie?

MARIE: The piece says consistent face. Across six angles. Consistent how. By what measurement.

RUTGER: I'll answer that one. In practice it's the same jaw, same eye spacing, same nose ridge, same hair pattern. Eyes that read as the same person across the panel.

MARIE: That is "looks like." Not consistent. Looks like is a perception. Consistency is a property of a sample over a distribution.

DINO: She is correct.

RUTGER: She is correct.

MARIE: I have a metric, by the way. If you want one.

RUTGER: Please.

MARIE: Pick the face. Pick fifty generations of the same prompt with a small variation. Compute the embedding cosine distance between every pair. If the mean cosine is above zero point nine and the variance is below zero point zero zero five — you have a consistent character. Below that, you have, hm, a *theme*.

DINO: That is more honest than the piece is.

RUTGER: I will steal this.

MARIE: Cite me.

RUTGER: Cited.

DINO: You see? She has done in one minute what your article did in eight.

RUTGER: I will accept the criticism.

DINO: I am still going to argue my point. You cannot use her metric to fix mine.

RUTGER: Go on.

DINO: Even if Marie's number is at zero point nine eight, you have only a consistent *face*. You still do not have a *character*. I will give you an example. The face of my grandmother. If you took thirty photographs of her, the cosine of her face would be zero point nine nine. The character of my grandmother — the way she laughed, the slight pause before she said anyone's name, the small joke she made about the priest at her own funeral, that she planted in her will — none of that is in her face. None of it is in the photographs. The character is somewhere else.

RUTGER: That is the best argument I have heard against the piece.

MARIE: I agree with him.

DINO: Did you just agree with me.

MARIE: I agreed with the argument. Not with you personally.

DINO: A small but important distinction.

MARIE: I am precise.

RUTGER: Let me try a defense. The piece is not claiming the character sheet is the character. The piece is claiming the character sheet is the *anchor*. Once you have the anchor — the face that the model recognises across generations — you can give the character a way of holding their hands by *prompting* it. The character library is the noun. The pose, the expression, the room, the situation — those are the verbs. The article is about not having to renegotiate the noun every time you write a verb.

DINO: That is a better argument than the article made.

MARIE: I would like the article to add a sentence: "the character sheet is the anchor; the character is what you do with it." This sentence saves the piece.

DINO: I agree with her, again. I am beginning to be annoyed.

RUTGER: Marie, you are getting all the writing credits today.

MARIE: I am precise. I am also fast.

DINO: She is also fast. This is the problem.

RUTGER: Last question. Dino. Forty years of art directing. What is the *real* failure mode of an AI character library, in your view.

DINO: The failure mode is that the team falls in love with the catalogue and stops casting. You spent two weekends making your character sheet. You bonded with it. Now every commercial you make for the next eighteen months has the same character in it because you have already paid the prep tax for that one. Suddenly the agency has a stable of three faces. Three. Forever. And the audience starts noticing. Why is the man from the bank commercial also in the insurance commercial and also in the pension commercial.

RUTGER: That is a real risk.

DINO: It is the same risk we had when stock photography arrived. We solved that by being annoyed about it for ten years and then learning to use it properly. We will solve this one the same way. Eventually.

MARIE: Eventually meaning?

DINO: Twelve years.

MARIE: That is specific.

DINO: I have been doing this a long time.

RUTGER: Thank you both. I'm going to add Marie's sentence to the article. And, Dino, I'm going to add your stable-of-three warning.

DINO: A small price for our time.

MARIE: We accept payment in espresso.

DINO: She is learning.

MARIE: I am not learning. I am Polish.

DINO: A perfect answer.
