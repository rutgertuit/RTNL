# Episode 05 — The Evolution of Video Models, with Dino
#
# Format: 1:1. Rutger walks Dino (creative dinosaur — Rotterdam art
# director, irritable, blunt, hates trends on principle) through the
# claim that generative video has crossed a real threshold with Veo and
# Omni. Dino spent forty years lighting rooms with tungstens. He is
# not going to be flattered.
#
# Length target: ~6 minutes (~1100 words).
# Tone: clipped, blunt, comic in its grumpiness. The pleasure is in
# Dino being slightly more right than Rutger expected.

RUTGER: Dino. Welcome.

DINO: Hm.

RUTGER: Coffee?

DINO: Espresso. Single. Real espresso. Not whatever your machine makes.

RUTGER: It is the same machine you have at the agency.

DINO: I know. That is why I am specifying.

RUTGER: Okay. So — I wrote a piece called The Evolution of Video Models. About Veo, the generative video tool from Google. The argument is that the same eight-second brief, four model generations apart, is now — well, indistinguishable from a decent live shoot. I wanted your take.

DINO: My take.

RUTGER: Yes.

DINO: My take is that you are confusing pixels with cinema.

RUTGER: Go on.

DINO: In nineteen ninety-six I lit a Volvo commercial. Six tungstens. One smoke machine. A grip from Den Bosch named Pieter who had not slept in three days. The shot was a slow push-in on a steering wheel. Eight seconds. *Eight seconds* … it took us four hours.

RUTGER: I know.

DINO: You do not know. You read about it. You did not stand in the room. The room smelled like burnt felt because one of the tungstens was slightly too close to the dashboard. The director kept stopping us because Pieter's smoke was drifting wrong on take six. By take nine the smoke was correct. The shot is in the commercial. The smoke is in the shot. The smoke is in the shot because we *earned* it.

RUTGER: Mm. That is a real difference. I'm not going to take that away from you.

DINO: You cannot. It is not yours to take.

RUTGER: But.

DINO: Yes, but. [sighs] I knew there was a but.

RUTGER: Look — the argument in the piece is not that Veo can light a Volvo commercial. The argument is that for the *category* of work that used to fill the production schedule between the Volvo commercials — the corporate social-media spot, the third-tier brand reel, the AB-test alternates — that work used to keep a junior crew alive. That work, now, can be Veo. And that is a real shift even if you and I both wish it were not.

DINO: Mm.

RUTGER: You don't disagree.

DINO: I disagree with the framing. You said "even if you and I both wish it were not." Speak for yourself.

RUTGER: Fair.

DINO: I do not wish it were not. I am happy to lose the third-tier brand reel. I never wanted to do them. The juniors did them because they had to eat. Now they will have to eat differently. That is — listen, Rutger — that is fine. That is not a tragedy. The tragedy is when you and your generation pretend the third-tier reel was the *craft*. It was not the craft. It was the survival job … between the craft.

RUTGER: That is the most generous thing I have ever heard you say.

DINO: Do not get used to it.

RUTGER: But you also wrote me an email last week complaining that AI-generated video would "kill cinema." I quote.

DINO: I was drunk.

RUTGER: I see.

DINO: Slightly drunk. Half a bottle of De Garre … it was a Tuesday.

RUTGER: So the email was wrong.

DINO: The email was correct in the part where I said you were being naive about how fast the threshold moves. The email was wrong in the part where I said cinema would die. Cinema has been dying since nineteen twenty-eight. Cinema is, in fact … a corpse that prefers walking.

RUTGER: That is a sentence I am stealing.

DINO: Steal it. I have many.

RUTGER: Let me ask you a real question. When did you stop being able to tell, on a small screen, what was a real shoot and what was Veo?

DINO: April.

RUTGER: This April?

DINO: April of this year. I was watching a commercial on YouTube on my phone, with the sound off, and I had two thoughts at the same time. One — that is a beautiful flame on a candle. Two — I could not have lit that flame in this many takes. Three — wait, was that an AI flame.

RUTGER: [chuckles] That's three thoughts.

DINO: I was upset. I have been upset since April.

RUTGER: But the craft of lighting a real flame is not gone.

DINO: The craft of lighting a real flame is the same as the craft of carving a tobacco pipe. It still exists. There are still people who will pay for a pipe carved by hand. Just not many. And not enough to feed a junior pipe-carver in Den Bosch.

RUTGER: Right — and that's the argument the piece is actually about, I mean — under the Veo demos. Not the lamp. Whether the next Pieter has a way to eat.

DINO: That is the only argument that matters.

RUTGER: So what's the answer?

DINO: The answer is that Pieter is going to learn Veo. Pieter is, in fact, learning Veo right now, on his couch, in a Den Bosch flat that he can only afford because his mother died and left him the apartment. The next Pieter will be a Veo operator. The Pieter after that will be a prompt engineer who has never lit a real lamp. And in fifty years they will be lighting commercials we cannot imagine, using inputs we cannot imagine, and they will be entirely uninterested in our nostalgia.

RUTGER: I don't think that's dark. I think that's the whole thing — and we keep dressing it up as a debate about cinema so we don't have to say it plainly. It was never the Volvo spot. It was never the candle. It's whether the next Pieter — on the couch, in the flat, learning Veo tonight — has a way to eat while he figures out what he's actually good at. The survival job changed. The question underneath it … didn't.

DINO: … You've been waiting the whole hour to say that.

RUTGER: A little.

DINO: It was decent. Don't do it again.

RUTGER: So the next Pieter eats.

DINO: The next Pieter eats. Differently. On worse coffee than this, which is saying something … and in fifty years he'll be telling some idiot with a microphone that none of it was real craft — and he'll be wrong in exactly the way I'm wrong now. That's the job surviving. That's the part I respect.
