# Episode 05 — The Evolution of Video Models, with Dino (and Saar)
#
# Format: 1:2 panel. Rutger walks Dino (creative dinosaur — Rotterdam art
# director, irritable, blunt, hates trends on principle) through the
# claim that generative video has crossed a real threshold with Veo and
# Omni. Saar (famous Dutch actress, raspy/seductive, gloriously clueless)
# is the comic foil — she reads the whole topic as a threat to HER face.
# Dino spent forty years lighting rooms with tungstens. He is not going
# to be flattered, and he is certainly not going to be patient with Saar.
#
# Length target: ~7 minutes (~1300 words).
# Tone: clipped, blunt, comic in its grumpiness. Saar is seasoning —
# a handful of beats, errors corrected so the point still lands.

RUTGER: Dino. Welcome. And Saar — thank you for coming in.

DINO: Hm.

SAAR: Of course, darling. I love a podcast. I did one once, in Ibiza. We never aired it. Legal reasons.

RUTGER: Coffee, either of you?

DINO: Espresso. Single. Real espresso. Not whatever your machine makes.

RUTGER: It is the same machine you have at the agency.

DINO: I know. That is why I am specifying.

SAAR: Just hot water and lemon for me. For the voice.

RUTGER: Okay. So — I wrote a piece called The Evolution of Video Models. About Veo, the generative video tool from Google. The argument is that the same eight-second brief, four model generations apart, is now — well, indistinguishable from a decent live shoot.

SAAR: Vivo.

RUTGER: Veo.

SAAR: That's what I said. Vivo. I had a fragrance with them. No — that was Vivienne. [chuckles] Anyway.

DINO: [dryly] It is going to be a long morning.

RUTGER: Dino — your take.

DINO: My take is that you are confusing pixels with cinema.

RUTGER: Go on.

DINO: In nineteen ninety-six I lit a Volvo commercial. Six tungstens. One smoke machine. A grip from Den Bosch named Pieter who had not slept in three days. The shot was a slow push-in on a steering wheel. Eight seconds. *Eight seconds* … it took us four hours.

RUTGER: I know.

DINO: You do not know. You read about it. You did not stand in the room. The room smelled like burnt felt because one of the tungstens was slightly too close to the dashboard. The director kept stopping us because Pieter's smoke was drifting wrong on take six. By take nine the smoke was correct. The shot is in the commercial. The smoke is in the shot because we *earned* it.

SAAR: I did a Volvo. Or — was it Saab. There was a wind machine. They told me to look at the horizon like I'd just lost someone. I cried real tears, you know … take after take.

DINO: That is acting. We are talking about lighting.

SAAR: It's all the same picture, darling.

DINO: It is NOT the same picture.

RUTGER: [chuckles] Let's — let me steer. The argument in the piece is not that Veo can light a Volvo commercial. It's that for the *category* of work that used to fill the schedule between the Volvo commercials — the corporate social spot, the third-tier brand reel, the AB-test alternates — that work used to keep a junior crew alive. That work, now, can be Veo. That's a real shift even if we both wish it weren't.

DINO: I disagree with the framing. You said "even if you and I both wish it were not." Speak for yourself. I am happy to lose the third-tier brand reel. I never wanted to do them. The juniors did them because they had to eat.

RUTGER: That's the most generous thing I've ever heard you say.

DINO: Do not get used to it.

SAAR: So what you're saying is — the computer does the boring jobs, and the artists keep the beautiful ones.

RUTGER: That's — almost. It's not really about boring versus beautiful. It's about who's left holding the survival job when the survival job disappears.

SAAR: Mm … but will it do my FACE?

DINO: [sighs] Here we go.

SAAR: No, seriously. This — Vivo. Can it do my face? Without me? Could I be in a commercial in Rotterdam while I'm in Ibiza? Can it do my Ibiza thing? The little — [laughs] — the thing I do.

DINO: She has been awake for the whole conversation and absorbed nothing.

RUTGER: It's a fair worry, honestly — just aimed at the wrong floor of the building. Dino, you wrote me an email last week saying AI video would "kill cinema." I quote.

DINO: I was drunk.

RUTGER: I see.

DINO: Slightly drunk. Half a bottle of De Garre … it was a Tuesday. The email was correct in the part where I said you were being naive about how fast the threshold moves. It was wrong where I said cinema would die. Cinema has been dying since nineteen twenty-eight. Cinema is, in fact … a corpse that prefers walking.

RUTGER: That's a sentence I'm stealing.

DINO: Steal it. I have many.

SAAR: I was in a corpse film. Played the corpse. Very demanding — you'd be surprised.

DINO: [dryly] I would not.

RUTGER: Let me ask a real question, Dino. When did you stop being able to tell, on a small screen, what was a real shoot and what was Veo?

DINO: April. April of this year. I was watching a commercial on YouTube on my phone, sound off, and I had two thoughts at once. One — that is a beautiful flame on a candle. Two — I could not have lit that flame in this many takes. Three — wait, was that an AI flame.

RUTGER: [chuckles] That's three thoughts.

DINO: I was upset. I have been upset since April.

SAAR: So if it can do a candle … it can do me.

DINO: Saar —

SAAR: No, but listen — if nobody can tell the candle from the real candle, then nobody can tell ME from the real me, and then why do they need the real me, and then what do I — … what do I DO?

RUTGER: Okay. That — Saar, that's actually the question. Not "do I get to keep being famous." Underneath the vanity of it, you just said the true thing: what happens to the person whose face was the job. That's the whole piece. It's just not only about you.

SAAR: It's not?

RUTGER: It's whether the next person making things — the next person who shows up wanting to do this for a living — has a way to eat while the tool eats their starter job. You're worried about your face. The real version of that worry is Dino's grip. Pieter.

DINO: That is the only argument that matters. And she stumbled onto it sideways … which is worse than not getting there at all.

RUTGER: So what's the answer?

DINO: Pieter is going to learn Veo. Pieter is, in fact, learning it right now, on his couch, in a Den Bosch flat that he can only afford because his mother died and left him the apartment. The next Pieter will be a Veo operator. The Pieter after that will be a prompt engineer who has never lit a real lamp. And in fifty years they'll be lighting commercials we cannot imagine, using inputs we cannot imagine, entirely uninterested in our nostalgia.

RUTGER: I don't think that's dark. I think that's the whole thing — and we keep dressing it up as a debate about cinema so we don't have to say it plainly. It was never the Volvo spot. It was never the candle. It's whether the next Pieter — on the couch, in the flat, learning Veo tonight — has a way to eat while he figures out what he's actually good at. The survival job changed. The question underneath it … didn't.

DINO: … You've been waiting the whole hour to say that.

RUTGER: A little.

DINO: It was decent. Don't do it again.

RUTGER: So the next Pieter eats.

DINO: The next Pieter eats. Differently. On worse coffee than this, which is saying something … and in fifty years he'll be telling some idiot with a microphone that none of it was real craft — and he'll be wrong in exactly the way I'm wrong now. That's the job surviving. That's the part I respect.

SAAR: So Pieter is me. The little Pieter, on the couch. He's just — me, but with worse lighting.

DINO: [sighs] … Yes, Saar. For once. Exactly that.

SAAR: Beautiful. I'd play him. Get me Vivo.

DINO: [sighs] VEO.
