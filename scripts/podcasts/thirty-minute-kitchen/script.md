# Episode 02 — The Thirty-Minute Kitchen, with Frits
#
# Format: 1:1 conversation. Rutger walks Frits the Nestor through the
# article's argument: most enterprise-marketing underperformance is a
# kitchen-tuning problem, not a renovation problem. Frits represents
# every creative director who lived through the cigarette-ad era and
# thinks "thirty minutes" is a slur.
#
# Length target: ~6 minutes (~1100 words).
# Tone: light, dry, two old friends, NOT a tutorial.

RUTGER: Frits. Thanks for coming in. Coffee? Or something stronger.

FRITS: It is eleven in the morning, Rutger. The polite answer is coffee. The honest answer is that I have been awake since five and I am open to negotiation.

RUTGER: Coffee it is.

FRITS: Black. As black as the future of the industry, please.

RUTGER: Already? We haven't started.

FRITS: I am pacing myself.

RUTGER: Okay. So — I wrote a piece called The Thirty-Minute Kitchen. The argument is that most of the underperformance I see inside enterprise marketing is not a missing tool, it's a missing tuning. Three knives, sharpened. Not a new kitchen. I figured you would have opinions.

FRITS: Thirty minutes.

RUTGER: Yes.

FRITS: To make what, exactly.

RUTGER: Not to make the work. To tune the room before you start.

FRITS: Ah.

RUTGER: You read the piece?

FRITS: I read enough of it to be insulted. Then I read the rest of it and I was less insulted. Then I read it a third time and I was, I would say, only mildly insulted.

RUTGER: Mildly insulted is my professional zone.

FRITS: Listen. In nineteen ninety-four, I shot a Heineken spot. One spot. Sixty seconds. We were six weeks on it. Six weeks. Storyboards on the wall, three rounds with the client, casting, location scout, smoke machine, a kid in Antwerp who knew how to mist a beer bottle so the condensation looked like a Dutch summer instead of a German one. There is a craft. There is a tempo. And then you come along and you say, the kitchen — the kitchen — can be tuned in thirty minutes. I felt it as a personal injury.

RUTGER: Did you finish the article.

FRITS: I finished the article. Yes.

RUTGER: And.

FRITS: The cook in your story did not cook faster. He arrived with his own three knives. He sharpened them on the counter. He moved one cutting board so the prep line was straight. And then he cooked for two hours, like a normal person.

RUTGER: Correct.

FRITS: So the thirty minutes is not the meal.

RUTGER: No.

FRITS: The thirty minutes is the part before the meal.

RUTGER: Yes.

FRITS: Then why did you call it thirty minutes? You are a marketer, Rutger. You know what a number does in a headline. You put a thirty in front of a kitchen, and the whole industry reads it as: the meal is thirty minutes.

RUTGER: That's fair. The article is more cautious than the title is.

FRITS: The article is always more cautious than the title is. The title is what people quote at me in the elevator at Adformatie events. The article is what I read on the train home, alone, with a small glass of port, while the man across from me eats a sandwich. Different audiences entirely.

RUTGER: Okay. Title aside. What's the part of the argument that lands for you?

FRITS: The three knives. That part is, hm, fine. I had three knives. I still have three knives. They are in a roll in my bag. I did not tell you that.

RUTGER: I assumed.

FRITS: One paring, one chef, one slicer. I would die for these knives. I will probably die because of these knives, eventually, at the security check at Schiphol.

RUTGER: The metaphor in the piece is that the three knives are clean data, structured documentation, and an AI workflow that connects to your execution layer without a human in the middle. That's the prep line. That's not the meal.

FRITS: Mhm.

RUTGER: What's the part that doesn't land.

FRITS: The renovations. You say agencies do a renovation when they should be sharpening a knife. I understand the metaphor. But you forget that some of the renovations happened because the kitchen was, in fact, on fire.

RUTGER: Sometimes.

FRITS: Often. We had a client, Smirnoff, around two thousand and eight. The kitchen was, by which I mean the agency, completely on fire. Three account directors had quit in eight months. Strategy had been outsourced to a consultancy in Hamburg who insisted on calling the brand "the product". A renovation was the only honest answer. You cannot sharpen your way out of that.

RUTGER: Agreed. The piece allows for renovations. It just notices that most teams jump to renovation when the actual problem is the salt is in the wrong cupboard.

FRITS: The salt is in the wrong cupboard. Yes. I will give you this one. I have, in my life, watched a thirty-million-euro media review get triggered because a CMO could not find a single number in a dashboard. He renovated the entire stack. The number was in the next tab.

RUTGER: That is the article in one sentence.

FRITS: Hm.

RUTGER: Hm?

FRITS: I am thinking.

RUTGER: Take your time.

FRITS: The cook in your story. He does not work in less time than the previous cook. He works in roughly the same time. He just does not waste two of the hours fighting the room.

RUTGER: Right.

FRITS: So the gain is not speed. The gain is the absence of friction.

RUTGER: That is a much better way to say it than I said it.

FRITS: Yes. I would like a credit on the next version of the article.

RUTGER: You're getting a credit on the podcast.

FRITS: I am suspicious of podcasts. The medium has a tendency to flatter the host.

RUTGER: I'll let that one pass.

FRITS: Generous.

RUTGER: One last thing. The piece says the answer is rarely an RFP. You spent forty years in agencies — what is your take on that.

FRITS: My take is that the RFP is the renovation. The RFP is the moment the CMO has decided to tear down the wall instead of sharpening the knife. By the time the RFP is on the street, three honest conversations would have solved seventy percent of the problem. But three honest conversations do not have a budget line, do not have a procurement department, and do not have a single internal owner. So we tear down the wall. Every time.

RUTGER: That is bleaker than my article.

FRITS: I am older than your article. That is allowed.

RUTGER: Frits, thanks for coming in.

FRITS: Was that thirty minutes?

RUTGER: Forty-two.

FRITS: A renovation, then.

RUTGER: A small one.

FRITS: A small one. Yes.
